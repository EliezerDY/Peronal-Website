Declaration of Independence

While everyone knows that the Declaration of Independence was signed on July 4, 1776, not everyone may realize that there were individual declarations before that. Starting in 1775, each state declared itself independent before they all came together as one to signal their removal from British rule.

War

However, it is one thing to declare oneself independent, and another to be able to make it so. This was not a "Bloodless Revolution." The Patriots took on a long and hard fight to be free of British occupation. After kicking the British out of Boston in 1775, the Americans controlled all the colonies. However, the British came back to New York and defeated the Colonial forces led by George Washington. They held this ground until the end of the war. New York remained under British control. However, an army marching from Canada under General John Burgoyne met superior forces, and he surrendered at Saratoga in 1777. After the Americans proved they could win, they were aided by the French who saw an opportunity to strike at their old foe, Great Britain. Washington's troops also received training from Prussian general Baron Friedrich von Steuben. When the British army in the South led by General Cornwallis became trapped in Yorktown, Virginia, with his back to the sea and troops led by Washington and Nathaniel Greene in front of him, he surrendered. That unofficially ended the war. The British had had enough. Even though they still maintained a strong force in New York, they agreed to let the colonies free in 1783 in the Peace Treaty of Paris.
